Learn how to stay safe on the internet

The internet is useful for learning, games, videos, messages, shopping, banking, and work. But it also has fake websites, phishing emails, suspicious downloads, weak passwords, and tricks designed to make you panic.

Why internet safety matters

Internet safety means protecting your accounts, private information, photos, messages, money, and devices. A scammer may pretend to be a bank, delivery company, support team, game platform, or shopping website.

A strong password is one of your best protections. A weak password like 123456789, qwerty, or password is easy to guess. A safer password is longer, uses different types of characters, and avoids obvious patterns.

Good safety is not about being scared of the internet. It is about slowing down, checking details, and not giving private information just because a page or message asks for it.

Big Safety Tips

Use strong passwords

A strong password is long, unique, and difficult to guess. Use uppercase letters, lowercase letters, numbers, and special symbols. Avoid names, birthdays, repeated letters, simple number patterns, and common passwords like 123456789 or password.

Do not reuse the same password everywhere. If one website gets hacked, attackers may try the same password on your email, bank, games, or social media accounts.

Check links before clicking

A suspicious link may look close to the real website, but small changes can reveal the trick. For example, netflix.com is not the same as netflx-help.com. Always look at the full domain before you click.

Be extra careful with messages that say you must act now, pay quickly, claim a prize, or verify your account immediately. Urgency is one of the most common phishing tricks.

Do not share private info

Never send passwords, card numbers, phone numbers, login codes, or ID details through random forms, pop-ups, messages, or emails. Real support teams should not ask for your password.

If a page asks for personal information too early, stop and check who owns the site. Open the official website yourself instead of trusting a link from a message.

Use two-factor login

Two-factor authentication adds a second step when logging in. Even if someone steals your password, they may still need a code from your phone or authenticator app.

Use two-factor login for important accounts like email, banking, GitHub, social media, and work tools. Your email is especially important because it can reset many other passwords.

Different Types of Phishing

Email phishing

Fake emails pretend to be from trusted companies and push you to click links or open files.

Smishing

Phishing through SMS messages, often about packages, bank alerts, or urgent payments.

Vishing

Phone-call phishing where someone pretends to be support, a bank, or another authority.

Spear phishing

Targeted phishing that uses your name, job, school, company, or personal details.

Clone phishing

A real message is copied, but the link or attachment is replaced with a dangerous one.

QR phishing

Scammers use QR codes to send you to fake login or payment pages.
