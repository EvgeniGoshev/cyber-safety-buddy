Why internet safety matters

Internet safety means protecting your accounts, private information, photos, messages, money, and devices. A scammer may pretend to be a bank, delivery company, support team, game platform, or shopping website.

A strong password is one of your best protections. A weak password like 123456789, qwerty, or password is easy to guess. A safer password is longer, uses different types of characters, and avoids obvious patterns.

Good safety is not about being scared of the internet. It is about slowing down, checking details, and not giving private information just because a page or message asks for it.

Big Safety Tips

Use strong passwords

Make passwords long, unique, and difficult to guess. Use uppercase letters, lowercase letters, numbers, and special symbols. Avoid repeated letters, simple number patterns, birthdays, names, months, years, and common passwords.

Do not reuse the same password everywhere. If one website leaks your password, attackers may try it on your email, games, social media, and bank accounts.

Check links before clicking

Scammers often use links that look close to real websites. A fake Netflix link may use netflx-help.com instead of netflix.com. Always look at the full domain before clicking.

Be careful when a message says something is urgent, limited, blocked, or about to be deleted. Pressure is one of the biggest scam tricks.

Do not share private info

Never send passwords, card numbers, phone numbers, login codes, or ID details into random forms, pop-ups, or messages. Real companies should not ask for your password.

If a page asks for personal data too quickly, close it and open the real website yourself by typing the address.

Use two-factor login

Two-factor authentication adds a second step when logging in. It can protect you even if someone learns your password.

Use an authenticator app or phone code for important accounts like email, banking, GitHub, social media, and school or work accounts.

Different types of phishing

Email phishing

Fake emails pretend to be a real company and try to make you click a link or open an attachment.

Smishing

Scam text messages often pretend to be delivery services, banks, or prize messages.

Vishing

Phone call scams try to pressure you into sharing codes, passwords, or payment details.

Spear phishing

A targeted scam uses personal details to sound more believable.

Clone phishing

A real-looking message is copied, but the link or attachment is replaced with something dangerous.

QR phishing

A fake QR code sends you to a dangerous website that asks for login or payment information.
